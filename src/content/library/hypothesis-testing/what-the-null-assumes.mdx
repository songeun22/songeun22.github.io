description: Before any test statistic, there is a claim being entertained, and it is a strange one.

Every significance test starts by taking seriously a claim nobody believes: that the effect you are looking for is exactly zero.

This is the null hypothesis, and the word *exactly* is doing an enormous amount of work. Not "small". Not "negligible". Zero, to infinite precision. It is a claim that is almost certainly false before you collect a single data point, and the entire machinery is built on top of it anyway.

## Why entertain something false

The null is not there because it is plausible. It is there because it is **specific enough to compute with**.

"There is some effect" does not tell you what the data should look like. "There is no effect" does, pinning down a distribution, and once you have a distribution you can ask how surprising your data is under it. The null is a fixed point you push against, not a belief you hold.

<blockquote>
The null hypothesis is a device for generating a distribution, not a description of the world.
</blockquote>

## What comes bundled with it

The zero-effect claim is the part people state out loud. It never travels alone:

- **Independence**, each observation carries new information, not an echo of the last one.
- **A sampling model**, some assumption about how the data were generated, often normality, or enough samples for the CLT to stand in for it.
- **A fixed rule**, the test, the sample size, and the threshold were decided *before* the data were seen.

When a test misleads you, the zero is usually not the culprit. One of these three is.
