A sample mean is not the truth. Run the study again and you get a different one. The sampling distribution is the shape of that variation, what your estimate would do across all the studies you did not run.

Everything downstream is a reading off this curve.

## Two curves, not one

The null gives you one curve: sample means centred at zero, spread by the standard error. But there is a second curve you rarely see drawn, the one centred at the *true* effect. Significance is decided by the first. Whether you will detect anything is decided by the second.

Three things are worth doing before reading on:

1. **Set the effect to 0.** The curves land on top of each other. Power collapses to α, and you reject at exactly the rate you agreed to be wrong. That is what α *is*.
2. **Hold the effect and drag <em>n</em>.** Both curves narrow. The critical line slides toward zero, and power climbs even though the true effect never changed. Sample size does not make effects bigger; it makes them **visible**.
3. **Loosen α.** The line slides left, power rises, and you have bought that power entirely with false positives.

## What the standard error actually controls

The spread of the null curve is $\text{SE} = \sigma/\sqrt{n}$, and that square root is the whole economics of study design. Halving your uncertainty costs four times the data. This is why underpowered studies do not merely fail, they fail *expensively*, and the fix is rarely a few more subjects.

## The trap

Notice that the critical line is placed using **only the null curve**. The test never consults the alternative. It cannot tell you whether an effect is real, only whether your data would be surprising in a world where it is not.

That gap is where nearly every misreading of a p-value lives, and it is what the next chapter is about.
